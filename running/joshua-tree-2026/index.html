BULLETPROOF THE RIGHT FOOT

Mid-portion Achilles · posterior tibialis · plantar fascia · matched week-for-week to the run plan · strength on Mon & Wed (after easy runs), light holds on Fri (after the long run)
How to read it. 3×10 = 3 sets of 10 reps. each = one leg at a time (per foot). Tempo like 3-2-3 = seconds up · hold at top · down — moving that slowly is the exercise. Default tempos: A 3-2-3, B 3-1-3, C 2-1-3. load = backpack or dumbbells. ISO = isometric hold (see below). Rest 60–90s between sets. The whole point of this layout: heavy eccentric work only on your two easy-run days; Friday after the long run is light holds only — never stack heavy calf work on long-run legs.

The exercises — exactly how

ISO · Isometric calf hold Pain relief + tendon stiffness

Same setup as A (ToePro, toes elevated). Rise to about halfway up and hold dead still at that height for the full count — no bouncing, no lowering. Both legs early on; one leg once it's easy. Push hard enough that it's a real effort but pain stays ≤3/10. 4–5 × 30–45s, rest ~60s. These calm an irritable tendon and keep it primed without fatigue — that's why they're the only thing on long-run Fridays and in race week.

A · Standing single-leg heel raise, toes elevated Achilles (mid) + plantar fascia

On the ToePro with the balls of your feet on the ramp so your toes sit higher than your heels (this pre-tensions the plantar fascia). Hold a wall/rail for balance only. Rise straight up onto the ball of the foot (3s up), pause at the top (2s), then lower slowly (3s down) until the heel drops below the platform into a gentle stretch — full range is fine for your mid-portion Achilles. Cue: rise straight up over the big toe, don't let the ankle roll outward (this protects the posterior tibialis). Progress: both legs → single leg → add a loaded backpack/dumbbell.

B · Seated heel raise Soleus — the downhill protector

What you already do. Sit, balls of feet on the ToePro/step edge, weight(s) resting on top of your knees, knees bent ~90°. Push up onto the balls of the feet (2–3s), pause (1–2s), lower slowly (3s) to a full stretch. The bent knee isolates the soleus — the biggest plantarflexor in running and the key Achilles protector on downhills. Progress by adding weight.

C · Resisted inversion Posterior tibialis (below the inner ankle)

Sit with the leg out, loop a resistance band around the forefoot, anchored so it pulls your foot outward. Keep the heel still and turn the sole inward and down against the band (2s), then control back (3s). No band? Do single-leg heel raises with a rolled towel or small ball squeezed between your inner ankles — it forces you up over the big toe and biases the posterior tibialis.

D · Pogo hops (from wk 16) Elastic / spring capacity

Small, fast hops in place off the balls of your feet — stiff ankles, minimal knee bend, quick ground contact, like skipping rope. Double-leg 3×20 → progress to single-leg sets of 10–15. This builds the tendon's spring so it tolerates faster running and downhills (where your "threat" shows up).

Pain rule. Up to 5/10 during the exercise is OK if it's back to normal by the next morning and isn't getting worse week to week. If morning pain climbs, you limp, or it sharpens — drop back one level (less weight / both legs / fewer reps). Don't push through.
General training info, not medical advice. These symptoms are recurring — if pain worsens or doesn't settle, get a hands-on assessment from a physio or sports-medicine clinician.
